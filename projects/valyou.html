Valyou

A single-input tool that translates dollar amounts into multiple life contexts simultaneously

The Problem

Digital payments make money abstract. When you're holding physical cash, the value is tangible. You can see the stack getting smaller, you feel the weight of what you're spending. But when you see "$150" on a screen, it's just a number. Your brain doesn't process "$150" the same way it processes "4 hours of work" or "30% of your laptop fund" or "enough to pay off a credit card." The number itself is meaningless without context. I noticed I'd hesitate to buy practical things like dishwasher pods or Advil because they felt expensive, but I'd spend the same amount on something completely frivolous without thinking. In different contexts, the same dollar amount seemed to have different values.

I built Valyou to make digital money feel concrete again. The problem wasn't budgeting, it was that I had lost all sense of context as to what a dollar amount actually represents in my life.

Traditional financial tools don't solve this. Budgeting apps track spending and set limits, but they still show you numbers. Balance sheets are just more numbers. Numbers don't trigger emotional responses. You need context to make a number meaningful.

The Solution

Valyou translates dollar amounts into context through a single-input, multiple-output interface. Enter any amount and the dashboard instantly reframes it across four contexts: Earnings, Investments, Goals, and Debts.

Type in $1,000. Earnings shows you worked 26 hours for that money. If you spread your salary across all your time instead of just work hours, it took 78 hours of your life to accumulate it. Investments shows this is 2.1% of your portfolio, or 1.67 shares of QQQ. Goals shows you're 67% of the way to a new Studio Display. Debts shows you can pay off 42% of your Chase balance. That same number has four different meanings, all of which are displayed simultaneously.

The tool includes a live earnings counter that ticks up in real-time based on your amortized salary. Watching it climb while considering a purchase creates immediate friction. There's also a Wins Tracker that displays your three most recent smart money decisions. Seeing that you used $800 toward your credit card balance reinforces you to make a smart financial decision.

This is not a budgeting tool. It doesn't discourage spending. It puts context at your fingertips so you can make informed decisions.

The Interface

The Master Input sits at the top in a giant font because everything else is secondary. Enter a number and the entire dashboard recalculates instantly. After initial setup, the only interaction is changing that input or locking an amount as a win.

The Earnings pane breaks time down two ways. Work Hours shows actual desk time (33.4 hours). Life Time shows calendar time including sleep and weekends (5d 20h). The Income Rates table calculates your rate down to the minute. Session Earnings ticks up in real-time while you sit there, making every minute feel accountable.

Each pane shows coverage percentages. The $1,888 input shows "86.7% coverage" on the Chase card, "107.7% coverage" on New Windshield. Makes comparison instant. Progress bars are just colored rectangles, the bar itself shows scale immediately.

Recent Wins shows your last three smart money decisions. These aren't avoided purchases, they're actual uses of money you locked as wins. Positive reinforcement for good decisions, not guilt about spending.

Stack: Vanilla JavaScript, Tailwind CSS, LocalStorage (client-side only)

Core Mechanic: Single Input, Multiple Output (SIMO)

Summary

The tool does one thing: it makes dollar amounts feel concrete. "$1,000" stops being an abstract number and becomes "26 work hours" or "2.1% of portfolio" or "67% of Studio Display goal" or "could pay off 42% of Chase balance." That context empowers users to make better financial decisions.
